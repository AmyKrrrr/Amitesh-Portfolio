Currently, I am a 2nd-year CSE student at a private university. Recently, I got an unexpected opportunity to witness a live placement interview session for final-year CSE students from a tier-2 NIT. My girlfriend, who is a Placement Coordinator there, allowed me to join the meeting using her ID, and I attended the entire interview process.

The company was based in Tamil Nadu and was offering a package of around **10 LPA CTC**.

To be honest, the experience completely changed the way I look at preparation for software roles.

A lot of candidates mentioned projects on their resumes that they had clearly copied or built without truly understanding. When the interviewer asked even basic follow-up questions about their own projects, many struggled to explain the simplest concepts.

One candidate claimed to have implemented Machine Learning in his project, but when asked about the difference between **online learning** and **batch learning**, he could not answer.

What surprised me even more was that some candidates could answer difficult questions but failed at relatively simple ones. A few of them seemed to understand concepts internally, but they lacked the ability to express their thoughts clearly.

Many were also unable to answer theoretical questions related to technologies they had already mentioned on their resumes, such as the **Event Loop** in NodeJS.

For every candidate, the interviewer asked around **4–5 questions**, and honestly, I barely knew the answers to most of them. *(It scared me.)*

### What I Learned

**1. Never Take CS Fundamentals Lightly**

For smaller and mid-scale companies, DSA is important, but it is not everything.

Understanding of:
- Operating Systems
- Networking
- Databases
- JavaScript internals
- System Design fundamentals

matters a lot.

**2. Never Fake Knowledge on Your Resume**

If you write something on your resume, be prepared to explain it deeply.

Interviewers can easily identify whether you genuinely built something or just copied it from somewhere.

**3. AI Can Build Projects for You, But Not Understanding**

In the era of AI, it has become very easy to generate code and create projects quickly.

The project may work perfectly, but if you do not understand:
- how it works,
- the concepts behind it,
- why certain technologies are used,

it becomes obvious during interviews.

**4. Communication Matters**

Some candidates actually knew the answers but failed to explain them properly.

Technical knowledge alone is not enough, being able to communicate your thought process clearly is equally important.

**5. Interviewers Often Ask From the Topics You Ignore**

We often skip “small” topics like:
- Promises
- Event Loop
- Caching
- OS fundamentals

assuming they are less important.

Ironically, those are the exact areas interviewers use to test depth of understanding.

This interview was honestly a wake-up call for me.

As a 2nd-year student, I realized how much there still is to learn, not just DSA, but actual computer science fundamentals and real understanding of the technologies we use every day.
